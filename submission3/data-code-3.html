Question 4: Briefly discuss what policies or events might explain the trends you observe. Why might we expect Medicaid expansion to affect uncompensated care?¶

The trends we see here, specifically the sharp drop in Expanded and the continued rise in Never Expanded uncompensated care costs, are mainly driven by the implementation of the Affordable Care Act (ACA) and the specific mechanisms of Medicaid expansion. Another factor is the Supreme Court ruling in 2012 that made expansion optional, contributing further to differences across states.

We expect a negative relationship because Medicaid acts as a financial buffer. In non-expansion states, the Never Expanded uncompensated care rises because these hospitals are still treating the same number of uninsured patients, but the cost of treating them is increasing over time due to medical inflation. In expansion states, the government takes those costs off of hospitals.

Question 10: Summarize your findings from questions 5-9. What is the effect of Medicaid expansion on hospital uncompensated care? Do the event study graphs suggest any concerns about the parallel trends assumption? Briefly discuss one limitation of the standard TWFE estimator in the context of staggered Medicaid expansion.¶

  • Medicaid expansion consistently reduced hospital uncompensated care costs from its implementation onward.
  • Using TWFE versus DD model increased the Adjusted $R^2$, showing that hospital-specific and year-to-year factors account for most of the cost variation.
  • Event study graphs validate parallel trends assumption, as pre-expansion coefficients were flat and statistically insignificant, showing no divergence before the policy implementation.
  • One limitation of the standard TWFE estimator is that it might underestimate the true effect due to the weighting issues that come up when comparing early-adopters to late-adopters. The TWFE estimator assumes the treatment effect is constant, but since Medicaid's impact tends to grow over time, comparing new expanders to old expanders can create a downward bias.
